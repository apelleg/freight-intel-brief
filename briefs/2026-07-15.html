🚛 Uber Freight Intel Brief — 2026-07-15

Read time: ~4 minutes

⚡ Today's Top Signal

Trump proposed a 20% toll on all cargo transiting the Strait of Hormuz on Monday (July 13) — then scrapped it Tuesday (July 14) under Gulf ally pressure, replacing it with pledges of direct Gulf state investment in the US. The 24-hour reversal crystallizes the operating environment: Hormuz geopolitics are producing policy whiplash at weekly cadence, the US Iran blockade is back on, and oil is up 10% from last week's brief respite. For freight, this isn't just an energy cost story — it's an import surge story. June container volumes hit 2.4M TEU (+8.2% YoY) with China imports up 27.4% as shippers front-loaded ahead of the Section 301 tariff deadline now nine days away (July 24). That cargo is clearing ports now and will translate into a domestic truck demand surge in the July 28–August 15 window, compounding a truckload market already running at its tightest since February 2022.

📋 Regulatory Watch

  • Section 301 tariffs: T-9 days (July 24 implementation): Following the July 7 public hearing, USTR is expected to impose 10–12.5% additional tariffs on 60 economies covering 99.4% of US imports, effective July 24 with no built-in expiry — replacing the Section 122 10% blanket tariff that expires the same day. Shippers are treating the deadline as a hard cliff: June container volumes are proof (+8.2% YoY, China +27.4%). Freight contracts written now have no stable tariff baseline; expect continued spot market pressure and a potential demand trough after July 24 as front-loading exhausts. (July 2026 — USTR / Movargo)

Third bilateral US-Mexico round (July 20, Washington DC) focuses on rules of origin in autos and energy, and China disciplines; Canada remains excluded. Cross-border freight uncertainty persists into 2027. (July 1, 2026 — USTR / Washington Post)

💡 CPO Lens: 3 Things Worth Discussing with Your Team

  1. The inbound container wave hits domestic TL in two weeks: June containers (+8.2% YoY, China +27.4%) are clearing ports now, bound for distribution centers in Inland Empire, Chicago, Dallas, and Atlanta over the next two weeks. With dry van already at $3.05/mile and climbing, this wave will hit into a market that has no slack. Is Uber Freight's carrier network positioned in those inland corridors with committed capacity, and is our dynamic pricing engine calibrated to capture margin in that demand spike — or are we leaving basis points to the spot board?
  2. Hormuz as a real-time 4PL differentiation moment: Trump's 20% cargo fee was proposed and scrapped in 24 hours — that's the pace of policy change shippers are navigating. With transpacific ocean at $7,500–9,000/FEU, some lanes and SKUs now favor domestic repositioning, air freight, or intermodal alternatives. Are managed-transportation customers on the Transplace platform getting proactive mode-shift recommendations when ocean economics shift this dramatically — or waiting for them to ask?
  3. TMS platform defense as ERP incumbents go agentic: Blue Yonder (19th consecutive Gartner TMS MQ Leader) and SAP (Joule agents inside existing ERP stacks) are both embedding autonomous TMS execution natively in systems large shippers already pay for. What specific Transplace capabilities justify enterprise renewal against "good enough" agents built into the ERP — multimodal cross-carrier optimization, 4PL control tower visibility, carrier relationship depth? Is there a partnership play with SAP or Snowflake (Blue Yonder's substrate) that strengthens our position rather than defending against it?
