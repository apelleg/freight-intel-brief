## 🚛 Uber Freight Intel Brief — 2026-07-21
**Read time: ~4 minutes**

### ⚡ Today's Top Signal
President Trump today signed three Section 338 proclamations imposing 50% additional tariffs on Canadian goods — covering autos, dairy, alcoholic beverages, wood products, paper, machinery, and textiles — effective August 16, 2026. Unlike every prior Trump tariff action, these explicitly apply to USMCA-qualifying goods, eliminating the preferential treatment that Canada-origin freight has relied on since 2020. For Uber Freight, this creates an urgent 25-day pull-forward window before August 16 during which Canada-US cross-border volumes will spike — followed by a hard demand cliff that could disrupt Transplace managed-transportation clients who run Canada-sourced supply chains. The timing stacks on top of Section 301 tariffs implementing in three days (July 24), making this the most compressed tariff-shock sequence of the year.

### 📋 Regulatory Watch
- **Section 301 tariffs — 3 days to implementation (July 24, 2026):** The 10–12.5% tariffs on 99.4% of US imports from 60 countries replace the SCOTUS-struck IEEPA rates and the now-expiring Section 122. Key freight implication: these tariffs have no time limit and no rate cap — 10% is the floor for the foreseeable future. JOC reports a Q3 booking freeze as shippers wait for compliance clarity; expect a 1–2 week air pocket in import volumes immediately post-July 24 before restocking demand resumes. (July 21, 2026 — [USTR](https://ustr.gov/about/policy-offices/press-office/press-releases/2026/june/ustr-makes-findings-and-proposes-action-60-section-301-investigations-relating-failures-take-action) / [movargo](https://www.movargo.com/post/post-after-july-24-section-301-tariff-2026))
- **Trump Section 338 tariffs on Canada — effective August 16, 2026:** Three proclamations signed today impose 50% additional tariffs on Canadian autos, dairy, alcoholic beverages, wood products, paper and paperboard, machinery, and textiles — covering ~$20 billion in imports. USMCA-qualifying goods are explicitly NOT exempt. Energy, potash, Section 232 goods, and critical minerals are excluded. Canada PM Carney called the action a "direct violation" of USMCA. Tariffs take effect in 26 days. (July 21, 2026 — [White House](https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2026/07/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-imposes-additional-tariffs-on-canada/) / [Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/7/21/trump-imposes-50-us-tariffs-on-some-canadian-goods-citing-discrimination))

### 💡 CPO Lens: 3 Things Worth Discussing with Your Team

1. **Canada Tariff Response (26-Day Clock):** With 50% Section 338 tariffs hitting Canadian autos, wood, paper, and machinery on August 16 — and USMCA goods not exempt — which Transplace managed-transportation clients have material Canada-origin exposure? Can we proactively model alternative sourcing lanes (Mexico, domestic) in Transplace TMS and offer that as a value-add this week, before competitors do?

2. **Section 301 Volume Cliff vs. Rate Opportunity:** The JOC is warning of a Q3 booking freeze as shippers pause import decisions awaiting Section 301 compliance clarity starting July 24. If spot volumes dip 1–2 weeks post-implementation, does Uber Freight's pricing algorithm need a manual override to avoid locking in capacity at peak rates during a temporary air pocket — and is this an opportunity to pre-sell favorable contract terms to shippers who'll need to ramp back up in August?

3. **Aurora Q2 Results (July 29) and AV Strategy Clarity:** Aurora confirms Gen-2 observerless deployment next week, and Uber Freight is a named customer with exposure to AUR's commercial ramp. The sister Waabi partnership (driver-as-a-service model) runs a parallel AV track. As CPO, do we need to formally define which AV partnership is primary for the Uber Freight product roadmap — and what does "AV-ready" freight execution look like in our TMS integration by year-end?
