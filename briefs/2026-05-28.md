## 🚛 Uber Freight Intel Brief — 2026-05-28
**Read time: ~4 minutes**

### ⚡ Today's Top Signal
Oil fell below $89/barrel on May 27 — for the first time since Iran closed the Strait of Hormuz in February — after Iranian state TV reported a 14-point draft framework was received by Tehran that would restore Hormuz shipping to normal within one month of signing. WTI fell to $88.90 before recovering; the White House called the Iranian TV report "a complete fabrication," and ADNOC's CEO assessed that even an immediate deal takes 4+ months to normalize actual oil flows. Diesel remains at $5.38/gal. For Uber Freight, this is the clearest early signal yet of potential rate structure disruption: the fuel surcharge component of current spot rates (~$0.65/mile per April data) is almost entirely Hormuz-driven. If talks close and diesel falls to $4.00–$4.40, the spot-contract spread recompresses and Uber Freight's margin window narrows materially. Scenario planning is warranted now.

### 💡 CPO Lens: 3 Things Worth Discussing with Your Team

1. **FDXF debut = 90-day LTL opportunity window:** FedEx Freight starts trading as an independent S&P 500 company on June 1 — the same day you start as CPO. Enterprise shippers who bundled FedEx parcel and LTL economics will need to restructure contracts under a newly independent FDXF facing margin pressure from public markets. What's Uber Freight's go-to-market plan to capture mid-market LTL accounts migrating off legacy FedEx structures, and does the product make LTL easy enough to trial alongside existing TL business?

2. **Hormuz deal = dynamic fuel surcharge is now a product requirement:** Oil markets already moved below $89 on deal rumors; actual diesel relief from $5.38 to $4.00–$4.40 could arrive in 30–120 days. Does Uber Freight's pricing infrastructure support dynamic fuel surcharge adjustment, or will UF be slow to pass savings to shippers while carriers capture the margin gap? Building a fuel-responsive rate structure is now a product priority, not a pricing ops question.

3. **USMCA rules-of-origin = cross-border product needs a supply chain advisory layer:** The bilateral round starting today in Mexico City will, over the next 6–12 months, shape whether nearshoring supply chains consolidate or fragment around the Mexico corridor. Does Uber Freight's cross-border product help shippers understand supply chain restructuring risk — or does UF only execute loads rather than inform the decisions that determine whether those loads exist?
